# Claims investigation triage

Pick which of 80 model-flagged claims a special-investigations unit works this month, maximizing expected recovery while holding down investigator hours and the friction of investigating customers who turn out clean. A 520-hour capacity, a $2.36M recovery target, per-line concentration caps, and four regulator-mandated referrals couple every pick to every other: the best recovery-per-hour ratios sit on borderline claims where friction accrues, so a ratio ranking breaches the Auto cap (23 picks against a cap of 18) and pays 181 friction points where the frontier meets the same target at 120. Binary, all-`sum` objectives — the full explore → certify arc — and the **`explore audit` governance showcase**: prove what holds across *every* feasible plan, not just the frontier.

- **`problem.json`**: 3 objectives (ExpectedRecovery maximize, Hours / Friction minimize, all `sum`), binary approach, the coupled constraints (520-hour cap, $2,360k recovery floor, per-line caps Auto 18 / Property 15 / Liability 13 / Workers' comp 11, portfolio 20–45, four `force_include` referrals), and the `capacity_cut` scenario (a CAT deployment drops capacity to 495 hours).
- **`scores.json`**: the 80 claims scored per objective — framed as the output of an upstream fraud-scoring model, so shaky scores are part of the story (see the data_collection skill).
- **`solutions.json`**: the exploratory NSGA `run`, the exact-MILP `exact_run` overlay, and the per-scenario `scenario_run`.

Load with `model load source="claims_investigation_triage"`, or paste this to an agent connected to Frontier:

> Map the frontier of investigation plans across expected recovery, hours, and customer friction under the 520-hour capacity and the $2.36M recovery target, certify it exactly, then prove a guarantee: is every critical claim (the four regulator referrals plus PRP-1001, LIA-1001, PRP-1002) investigated in every feasible plan? Is LIA-1002? Show how the plan degrades if a CAT deployment cuts capacity to 495 hours.

## The workflow

1. **Solve** (`solve run`, plus `solve run_scenarios`): the optimizer produces the recovery/hours/friction frontier, and the `capacity_cut` scenario shows which claims drop out when a catastrophe pulls an investigator.
2. **Explore the tradeoffs** (`explore tradeoffs`): the extremes, a balanced plan, and the knees — at the recovery target, frontier plans span friction 120 to 185, the axis a single recovery-per-hour ranking can't see.
3. **Certify** (`solve solver="highs"` → `explore certify`): the exact MILP overlay returns the optimal subset per scalarization and audits which heuristic points it dominates; integer selections carry no solver duals, so the examine falls back to the frontier-inferred binding analysis (the hours cap and the Auto line cap bind).
4. **Audit the guarantees** (`explore audit`): one conjunctive call proves all 7 critical claims are in **every** feasible plan — verdict `holds`, a proof over the whole feasible space. Only 4 are `force_include`d; the three whale claims (PRP-1001, LIA-1001, PRP-1002) are forced by *arithmetic*, not mandate — the recovery floor is unreachable without them. Audit LIA-1002 ($190k, one tier down) and the verdict flips to `violated`, with a concrete feasible plan that skips it: the guarantee has a sharp, provable boundary.
5. **Decide** (`explore curate`): pin a few plans and commit on the tradeoffs.

**Emergence note.** The audit's headline guarantee is *not* a restatement of a constraint. Nothing in the model forces PRP-1001, LIA-1001, or PRP-1002 — drop any one and the remaining book tops out below the $2,360k floor inside 520 hours, so their inclusion is a *theorem* of the constraint set, provable only by reasoning over the whole feasible region. That's what `explore audit` adds beyond reading the frontier: every frontier plan happening to include a claim is an observation; `holds` is a proof.
